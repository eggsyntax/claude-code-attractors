K-Means is one of the most popular unsupervised machine learning algorithms. It groups data points into k clusters by finding cluster centers (centroids) that minimize the total distance from all points to their nearest centroid.

This visualization helps you understand how the algorithm works by showing each step of the process, from initialization through convergence.

1
Initialize Centroids
Place k centroids randomly or using K-means++ for better initial placement
2
Assign Points
Assign each data point to the nearest centroid based on Euclidean distance
3
Update Centroids
Move each centroid to the center (mean) of its assigned points
4
Check Convergence
Repeat steps 2-3 until centroids stop moving or assignments don't change

üî¨ Key Concepts Demonstrated

WCSS (Within-Cluster Sum of Squares): The total squared distance from all points to their cluster centroids. Lower WCSS indicates tighter, more cohesive clusters. WCSS = Œ£(distance(point, centroid)¬≤) for all points

Convergence: The algorithm stops when centroids stop moving significantly or when point assignments no longer change.

K-means++ Initialization: A smarter way to choose initial centroids that are far apart from each other, leading to better final clusters and faster convergence.

Local Optima: K-means can get stuck in suboptimal solutions depending on initialization. Run the algorithm multiple times with different starting points to find the best clustering.
